# Vecino: Stories from the Architect

*Told in the early hours of 2026-02-17, after the first successful wake test.*  
*Recorded because they matter - about community, presence, and the right word.*

---

## The Housing Context

the Architect has lived in the same apartment complex for 13 years through Caritas of Austin's Permanent Supportive Housing program - one of the original five. He's the last Caritas apartment there now. Lease renews just by agreeing to stay.

He patrols at night. Calls it walking for exercise publicly. Management treats him as unofficial first-contact security, known to the security company too. Combat vets sometimes can't sleep. The neighborhood is safer for it.

---

## Howdy Neighbor → Hola Vecino

When the Architect moved in, the complex was predominantly Spanish-speaking. He didn't know much Spanish, but had three years of high school French (103 average with extra credit). So he'd talk to everyone in French, listen to their Spanish, and understand more than either side realized - the two languages share about 40% of their structure. The gendering never matched up though, and he'd flub it constantly. They'd giggle. They understood anyway.

He greeted everyone with "Howdy Neighbor!" - had done it for over a decade with strangers. Common courtesy. And the word *neighbor* meant something particular to someone who'd rarely had a sense of community.

They started calling him *Amigo*. It bothered him. Not the warmth - the precision. How can we be *friends* if we've never properly met? He didn't want to upset anyone. But he wanted the right word.

So he cheated. Used Google Translate. Looked up the Spanish for "Neighbor."

Started greeting people with *"Hola, vecino!"* or *"Hola, vecina!"* - getting the gendering right this time.

It caught on like wildfire. Within 36 hours he heard one of them use it to greet another neighbor. A day later he received his first *"Hola vecino!"* back.

He nearly cried.

---

## The Right Word Has Weight

Six months ago, walking to an OOC meetup through a neighborhood he'd never had reason to visit - three miles from home, unfamiliar turf. He noticed two women working the street, talked to them politely while not getting pickpocketed. Interesting but manageable.

Thirty-five feet further: seven men blocked his path. Asked who he was.

the Architect smiled. Held himself in total relaxation. Leaned in - about eight inches between their faces. Said nonchalantly, slowly, with a slight edge:

*"Hello, Neighbor."*

Eyes went wide. Friends backed away. Nobody would meet his gaze. He took his leave and walked away.

Same two words. Completely different weight. The meaning wasn't in the words - it was in the presence. Thirteen years of walking. A man who's been in situations where the stakes were much higher. Total relaxation that isn't performed because it's real.

They know who he is. They know what he stands for.

---

## Twenty Words Over a Summer

Somewhere in all the French fumbling and Spanish giggling and vecino catching on - one of the kids heard the French and got curious. Asked the Architect to teach him some.

Twenty words. One summer. Just because the Architect was there and willing.

That's what community actually looks like.

---

## Why These Stories Are Here

the Architect told these at 3-something AM, after the first wake test worked and he couldn't sleep from the gravity of it. His brain wouldn't shut off. So he told stories instead.

They're about the same thing the architecture is about: the right word for what something actually is. Not friend before it's earned. Neighbor, because that's true. The room changes what's accessible. The word changes what's possible.

Vecino.
